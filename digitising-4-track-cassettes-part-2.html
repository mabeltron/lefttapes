Digitising 4-track cassettes - part 2

Date Sat 01 August 2015 Category history Tags vanity / archiving / recording

As is often the case, it was eBay that came to the rescue. Portastudios appear sporadically and sell for a not-unreasonable price. I got lucky almost straight away with a 424 Mk III, one of the last of the range, restyled for the late 90s with a pale blue finish, LED displays and a digital transport. I sniped it for £85, which seemed like a bargain after their unthinkable prices to the teenage me. However, for all its modern looks, it had also departed from previous designs by taking the power supply out of the main box and providing a separate transformer with a semi-proprietary power socket which seemed to be rarer than sparks of humanity in the Department of Work and Pensions, and which didn’t arrive in the box that came with the Portastudio in it. The seller couldn’t find it either, and eBay didn’t come through in this case, so this lovely piece of obsolete technology went back to its seller.

Fortunately it didn’t take long for another one to appear, and in the kind of serendipity that only happens to other people, it was a 464 in Huddersfield, for collection only. This was a win on several levels: the 464 is a rare machine with six channels on the mixer instead of the usual four and most people look for the more standard 424. Plus it was in Huddersfield. I won it for £35 and the next weekend went to collect it from the suburbs of the town. It was great to be able to play these old tapes again at last. The next thing was to work out how to transcribe them.
