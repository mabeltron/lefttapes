LFT002: Flail: The White Mouse Will Not Explode

The mid-late 80s also saw the rise of bands, mostly American, who were happy to show their heavier rock influences. I can’t remember where I first came across Big Black but the idea that a band could play short, blunt and hard post-punk rock using a drum machine like a battering ram was enormously appealing. At the same time parts of the British music press were celebrating the Blast First label and to a lesser extent other American labels like Touch & Go and Homestead, who provided a rougher foil to fey British indie. At the same time, the UK had thrash and post-punk metal bands of its own, with Nottingham’s Earache records at its heart. I was never a great fan of much of their output, being either high speed noise or half speed Black Sabbath (I enjoy the latter a lot more now).

Godflesh were a revelation, a duo who played slow and heavy with a crunching drum machine, the missing link between Big Black and Killdozer, but Brummies, who still played slowed down Sabs riffs but were somehow better.

A couple of years later, in one of my short lived jobs, I worked with a man who one day mentioned that his son was in a band and they had just had a CD out, and would I like to hear it? Naturally I did, and they turned out to be Pitchshifter and it was their debut LP, ‘Industrial’. They discovered breakbeats and samplers later on and created an early variant of the sort of dance oriented metal that Pendulum and Hadouken! made popular in the mid-2000s, but I think it’s safe to say that ‘Industrial’ was a pitch(shifted) perfect tribute to Godflesh.

One morning, well into my recording frenzy, I started a day with nothing planned and then my desultory twiddling turned into a low E chug from which all the parts came together and ‘Fear Is the Key’ was born very quickly. ‘Whitey’s Place’ came quickly after, based on the Mother Riff itself, and the other two followed in the next day or so. Over 25 years later, I can remember doing it and enjoying putting them together, but knowing that there wouldn’t be any more and not knowing why they came out in the first place.

‘Fear is the Key’ came off a mix tape rather than the masters as it was bounced from four track to two track and back. The others were actually the most difficult things to mix properly as they were recorded so quickly and the levels were all over the place.
